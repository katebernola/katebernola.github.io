# Indefensible defaults

## What it looks like

A pre-selected plan labeled "Best value." A recommended option with no stated reason. A default showing a monthly price when the user will be charged for multiple months up front to get that price.

## Why it happens

- The business wants the default chosen, and a superlative feels more persuasive than a fact.
- The monthly framing tests better than the real charge amount — because it hides the real charge amount.

## The rule

A defaulted or recommended choice must be explained by a **legally defensible claim**: "most common," "cheapest," "includes your current plan's features." These are verifiable — anyone can check them.

"Best value" is technically acceptable but borders on a dark pattern — "value" is our judgment dressed as a fact. It crosses the line when the framing hides the commitment: a monthly price on an annually-billed plan is a claim about a number the user will never actually pay in that shape.

## How to catch it

- Can the claim be verified by someone outside the company? If not, it isn't defensible.
- Does the displayed price match the charge that will hit their account? If not, the default is selling, not defaulting.
- **Which number is visually loudest?** If a per-period teaser outranks the amount actually charged, the hierarchy is doing the deceiving — regardless of what the fine print says.
- Would the label survive being read aloud in a deposition? ("It was our most common plan" survives. "We felt it was the best value" does not.)

## The fix

Label defaults with the verifiable fact that earned them the default. And the real charge — the amount that will actually hit the user's account — is the **most prominent number in the visual hierarchy**. A monthly price may appear, but it never outranks the real charge in size, weight, or position. If the honest label and the honest number don't make the default attractive, the problem is the default, not the label.
