# Visual hierarchy

## Purpose

Who speaks first, and how loudly.

## Principles

- Position one gets read; everything after gets scanned, maybe.
- The first slot is the only one with guaranteed attention. Spend it on a need-to-act message.
- On surfaces with actions, the real reading order is title → button → act; body text is optional depth. The key takeaway must survive that path — put it in the title and button, even at the cost of brevity. See [Title plus button](title-plus-button.md).

## Rules

- Reading order: need-to-act messages first, support after.
- When money is involved, the most prominent number is the amount that will actually be charged. A per-period teaser price never outranks the real charge in size, weight, or position. See [Indefensible defaults](../mistakes/indefensible-defaults.md).
- Treatment follows position: lead is largest and first (body size or above, weight 600–700); second is body size, regular weight; support and tertiary are small (16px), muted, last or behind disclosure.
- A support message never outranks a lead message by size or position.

## Decision heuristics

- Read the order top to bottom as a user would. If each line sets up the next, narrative order can beat priority order — but choose it, don't drift into it.
- Two lead messages compete for position one: merge them or sequence them.

## Common mistakes

- Ranking by stakeholder importance instead of user need.
- Making everything bold, which makes nothing bold.

## Examples

- Upload error final order: what happened → the limit → your other files are safe.

## Counterexamples

- Awaiting one: a case where burying the lead was right.

## Exceptions

- Narrative order may lead with context when the action makes no sense without it — a deliberate, recorded choice.
