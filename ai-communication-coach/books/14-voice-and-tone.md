# 14 · Voice and tone

## Purpose

How the product sounds. Borrowed from public best-in-class systems until internal standards land — every rule carries its source; swap on access.

## Principles

- Voice is constant; tone adapts to the customer's mood and moment. *(source: Intuit voice & tone)*
- Voice attributes to hold: warm, confident, genuine, empathetic, simple, clear. *(source: Intuit voice & tone)*
- Don't overthink voice — focus on sounding human, and use the vocabulary users actually use. Read it aloud to check. *(source: Polaris fundamentals)*
- Around money and stress, tone work is empathy work: adjust reassurance and explanatory depth to the customer's emotional state. *(source: Intuit voice & tone)*

## Rules

- Address the user as "you"; the company is "we." Use "I" only when the user gives consent ("I agree to…"). *(source: Polaris grammar)*
- Use contractions — they're short and conversational. *(source: Polaris grammar)*
- Say "we"/"us" in an error only when we caused the problem. *(source: Polaris error messages)*
- Never "Oops" — it undermines confidence. No "sorry" as filler. *(sources: Intuit word list; Polaris error messages)*
- "Please" sparingly, for extraordinary requests only. *(source: Intuit word list)*
- At most one exclamation mark per page, for genuinely exciting content. *(source: Polaris grammar)*
- Prefer "your" over "my" in UI labels. *(source: Intuit word list)*
- Avoid "easy," "simple," "just," "only," "quick" in instructions — they discourage anyone who's struggling, and ease is subjective. *(sources: Polaris inclusive language; Intuit word list)*
- **Stay apolitical.** Watch for anything carrying political charge — contested holiday names, place names, loaded terminology — and take the neutral framing. Name a thing by the part the user needs: a payroll calendar's job is "banks are closed," not settling whose holiday it is. Picking either side of a contested name upsets half the audience and helps no one finish their task.

## Decision heuristics

- Panic words get calm replacements: "compromised" not "hacked/breached/leaked"; "stopped responding" not "crashed"; "issue" not "bug"; "suspicious activity" not "fraud." *(source: Intuit word list)*
- If the moment is tense (stage: moment of need, decision, leaving), strip playfulness entirely — cheerfulness during stress reads as not being taken seriously.

## Common mistakes

- Adjective-based reassurance ("don't worry, it's easy and safe!"). Reassure with facts.
- Apologizing instead of informing. See the upload-error case: the apology was cut; the specific fact stayed.

## Examples

- "Your other files are safe" — fact-based reassurance in the user's terms. See `../examples/before-after/upload-error.md`.
- A tax calendar listed "Indigenous Peoples' Day (Columbus Day)." Both names are contested, and neither is what the reader needs. "Bank holiday" is the fact the payroll user came for.

## Counterexamples

- Awaiting one: a moment where an explicit apology measurably helped. (Standing doubt logged in `../thinking/we-might-be-wrong.md`.)

## Exceptions

- Legal and consent language may break conversational rules. Contain it; don't let it set the screen's tone.
