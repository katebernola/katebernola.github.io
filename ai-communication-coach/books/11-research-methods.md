# 11 · Research methods

## Purpose

How evidence gets into decisions. Thin on purpose — this book grows as methods get used for real.

## Principles

- Tickets show where people got stuck, not what words will unstick them.
- Borrow the user's vocabulary. Their words beat ours.
- Define success before writing: name the behavior the copy should change and how you'll know.

## Rules

- **Interrogate the metric before reasoning from it.** What exactly is measured? Over what population? At which funnel stage? "30% vs 80%" means one thing as participation and a completely different thing as completion-of-started-flows — and the strategies they imply don't overlap. A number that doesn't make sense is a prompt to dig, not a premise to build on. (Learned the hard way: see `../reviews/retirement-popup-review.md`.)
- Before writing from a support-ticket request: read five real tickets. Note the words users use.
- Before shipping a copy change tied to metrics: write down the expected behavior change. Without it, any result will look like a win — or a loss — depending on who is asking.
- When a belief in `../beliefs/` gains or loses evidence, update its confidence field and say what the evidence was.

## Decision heuristics

- Session recordings answer "do they read it?" faster than debate does.
- A guess is fine, as long as it is labeled a guess.

## Borrowed evidence

Dialect-neutral findings worth citing in reviews (UK-specific spelling and vocabulary excluded):

- Plain language is preferred by high-literacy readers too: in GDS-cited research on specialist legal language, about 80% of readers preferred the plain-English version — and the more complex the issue, the stronger the preference. Useful when a stakeholder argues "our users are experts." *(source: GOV.UK / GDS content design)*
- GOV.UK writes to a reading age of ~9. That is not writing for 9-year-olds — it is writing for how everyone scans under load. Our grade-8 ceiling and Polaris's 7th-grade target sit in the same band. *(sources: GOV.UK content design; Polaris fundamentals)*

## Common mistakes

- Citing "research says" without a source. If there's no source, it belongs in `../beliefs/` with a confidence level, not in a book.
