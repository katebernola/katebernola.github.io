Step 1: Eliminate low-value work

Every team has some grunt work. This team had all grunt work. A little middle-school math proved my point: it didn't make cents for skilled designers to write Help content.

(See what I did there with cents?)

Case study

Help Center articles

8 hours to create

× $120 per resource-hour

$960 per article

$24 savings per contact avoided

× 5% chance one article view prevents a contact

$1.20 max value per view

$960 per article

÷ $1.20 max value per view

800 views to break even

Then we looked at what we'd already published:

Help Center articles* 1,203
Articles with 800+ views annually 382
Articles worth the cost to write 32%
Articles not worth the cost to write 68%

*Published at least a year ago.

What did we spend on articles without sufficient ROI?

Within the company 6,568 hours · $788k
Content design team 3,284 hours · $394k

The new plan

We added a serious gatekeeping mechanism to intake:

  • Content designers decided the MVP article set for each release. About 1 hour per two-week sprint, 26 hours a year.
  • Requests needed data showing enough search or chat activity on the topic, or they were rejected.
  • Stop adding types of articles where virtually zero met the criteria: release notes, product overviews, explanatory articles.

New article creation dropped 90%: from 20 per sprint to 2. Upkeep dropped too. We archived more articles than we fixed.

We proved something our UX research team had also seen. When users go to Help Center, they want one thing: step-by-step instructions for how to do something. And that's it. These numbers spoke louder than qualitative insights.

The best part? Step-by-step articles averaged about 4 hours to create, rather than 8.

More work we stopped

  • Dropped support for more than 200 Help Center videos. At 24+ production hours each, only 15 earned their place.
  • Handed demo video production back to marketing, after a template made the work easy to do well at a lower rate.
  • Negotiated with product to stop chasing error edge cases: ship a generic error, then invest only where trackers showed it actually happening.

Principle: Show the math. When leaders see that two-thirds of your output costs more than it returns, stopping becomes the obvious call.

Step 2: Show value

Freeing people up doesn't require new headcount. Point them at work where you know they'll make a difference.

I picked a few big, visible projects where I knew we'd make a real difference. One content designer rescued an investment rebalancing flow that had failed three rounds of usability testing, then rebuilt the retirement income estimator around how users actually think. The retirement space made him their full-time content designer, and demand grew from there.

Sometimes showing value means proving the problem isn't content at all. When a payroll team asked for "content support" on a workflow nobody could explain, I mapped the information architecture and showed that the real issue was system design. That redirected the team toward the project that actually needed UX early.

Principle: Find sure-fire wins. Avoid last-minute patches. Aim for "essential to success," not "superhero work."

Step 3: Scale value

Once you've proven impact, invest in systems: content standards written for product designers, reusable content, templates, decision maps, and clear intake processes. One person's expertise multiplies across the organization.

Step 4: Change the culture

Step 3 is the most work. This one is the hardest. You're asking people to think and work differently. You need permission, credibility, and consistency.

Every audience got its own plan. My designers got a shared goal, weekly 1:1s, and team rituals. Leaders got the plan worked into departmental and personal goals. All of UX got the vision, the early wins, and the path. Partners got relationships first, announcements second. Surveys measured how it was going, and I adjusted as we went.

Longer term, content designers embedded into each product team, staying connected through a community of practice. There were tears, but it was the right move.

Outcomes

  • Stopped work that cost more than it returned, and redirected the team to high-value projects
  • Built leadership credibility through visible wins; demand for content design grew across product teams
  • Established standards, templates, and tools that let partners get what they need without waiting on a specialist
  • Left the organization permanently more capable

Lessons learned

The culture change only worked where my designers pushed for it themselves. They had to tell product designers to include them earlier. They had to raise issues constantly. They had to press for changes like reusable components, so text didn't need updating in a million places. That kind of advocacy can feel like ratting on the same people you're trying to collaborate with, and it never stops. Where a designer was willing to do it, the culture shifted. Where one wasn't, everything slid backward.

One content designer kept waiting for someone else to tell his product team to work differently, instead of asking, reminding, and pushing his way in. Nothing shifted in his space. He ended up working like a UI writer, got handed more Help Center articles than anyone, and without the strategy half of content design, he couldn't show his work was worth more than the articles. The lesson: a leader can clear the path and back the team up, but each designer has to do their own pushing.
