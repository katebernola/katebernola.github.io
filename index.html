1

Step 1: Identify what doesn't add value

Do the math. Stop the work.

The move

Every team has some grunt work. My team had all grunt work. Middle-school math proved it didn't make cents.

Case study

Help Center articles

The math

8 hours to create

×$120 per resource-hour

$960 per article

$24 savings per contact avoided

×5% chance one article view prevents a contact

$1.20 max value per view

$960 per article

÷$1.20 max value per view

800 views to break even

The reality

Help Center articles 1,203
Articles with 800+ views annually 382
Articles worth the cost to write 32%
Articles not worth the cost to write 68%

The bill for the 68%

OrgHoursMoney
Company6,568$788k
Content design team3,284$394k

The fix

  • Content designers set the MVP article list for each release. 1 hour per sprint, 26 a year.
  • No search or chat data showing demand? Rejected.
  • Stopped article types where virtually none met the bar: release notes, product overviews, explainers.

The result

New articles dropped 90%: from 20 per sprint to 2. We archived more than we fixed.

When users go to Help Center, they want one thing: step-by-step instructions. And that's it.

Our UX research team had seen this for years. The numbers finally made it land. And step-by-step articles take about 4 hours, not 8.

More work we stopped

  • Multimedia team: dropped 200+ Help Center videos. Only 15 earned their place.
  • Multimedia team: sent demo video production back to marketing, with a template that made it easy to do well.
  • Product design: stopped chasing error edge cases. Ship a generic error; invest only where trackers prove it happens.

Principle: Show the math. When two-thirds of your output costs more than it returns, stopping is the obvious call.

2

Step 2: Find surefire wins

Let your people shine. Fuel demand.

The move

So people are bored. Now what? Find projects where the need is so obvious, sending in an expert almost feels like cheating.

Case study

The retirement cross-sell test

Not every surefire win is easy. This one was a fight — but with the data on my side, the outcome was never in doubt. A sales team was building an ad into the left navigation of a product companies pay thousands for, and it was already shipping. Most of the people who'd see it weren't prospects. They were payroll customers trying to run payroll.

The conflict

The plan added a new primary nav item labeled "Retirement," with a dot marking new content. It read as another product. Clicking it opened what was functionally an ad, routing users out of the platform to book a sales call.

The proposed treatment changed the platform's information architecture. A promotion was disguised as a product.

The evidence

Under 1% of users who saw it clicked through and finished. Of the ones who clicked, 78% closed it immediately. That's not a conversion problem — it's a trust problem. People thought they were clicking a product.

The test

Instead of arguing about taste, I designed four alternatives and ran all five at once as an A/B/C/D/E test. I reframed the message from a pitch ("free retirement plan, tax credits") to a compliance notice ("no retirement deductions detected — you may be out of California compliance"), set display rules so it never shadowed a real payroll message, and added a dismissal survey to learn why people hid it.

The surprise

The treatment that reached the fewest people won: a pop-up shown only at payroll submit. Context beat exposure. And the survey nobody asked for caught a flaw the business couldn't see — about a third of respondents already had retirement. The targeting had missed a state-specific earnings code, so the exclusion logic was quietly wrong.

The line

The business then asked for the list of users who opened the ad and closed it, on the logic that opening it signals interest. I refused, and won executive backing: a company pursuing product-led growth can't punish users — with spam or cold calls — for exploring a promotion it placed in its own interface.

The result

148 wins and over $520K in annual recurring revenue, with the campaign still running. The revenue wasn't the point — it was the proof. The user-protective choice and the revenue-maximizing choice turned out to be the same choice.

More wins

  • A 401(k) rebalancing flow had failed three rounds of usability testing. My designer reworked it, then rebuilt the retirement income estimator around how users think. Retirement made him their full-time content designer.
  • A payroll team asked for "content support" on a workflow nobody could explain. I proved the real issue was system design, and the team reprioritized toward the project that needed UX early.
  • Demand for content design grew across product teams.

Principle: Find surefire wins. No last-minute patches. "Essential to success," not "superhero work."

3

Step 3: Make great work repeatable

Build tools and frameworks. Multiply impact.

The move

Invest in systems: standards written for product designers, reusable content, templates, decision maps, clear intake. One expert multiplies.

Case study

AI content design coach

Scaling content design expertise across the organization.

We couldn't hire more content designers.

Instead, I helped make their expertise available to everyone.

When our product footprint nearly doubled, the obvious solution was adding people. We couldn't. At the same time, several content designers had grown into new roles across UX, leaving a smaller content design practice to support a much larger organization. The result: the content design practice was spending too much time rewriting repeatable UI copy instead of helping shape products and designing the next generation of AI-ready systems.

The goal wasn't to replace content design. It was to reserve content design expertise for work that required it.

The challenge

Years of content design expertise existed. Almost none of it scaled.

More than 120 pages of standards covered voice and tone, communication strategy, reusable patterns, component guidance, accessibility, and product messaging. New content designers took months to become fluent. Product designers, researchers, product ops, and marketing couldn't realistically learn it all. Yet all of them created product content.

The bottleneck wasn't writing. The bottleneck was access to expertise.

The strategy

AI was part of the solution, but it wasn't the solution. The solution required knowledge architecture.

Before training a model, we reworked our standards. Every component needed a clear purpose. Every recommendation needed a reason. Every strategy needed to be explicit instead of living in experienced content designers' heads. We removed documentation that didn't affect writing, clarified decision criteria, expanded guidance to include temporary product messaging, and structured the knowledge so both people and AI could understand it. Only then did we teach it to Copilot.

The AI coach

Rather than generating generic copy, the agent acted like an experienced content designer sitting beside you. It could:

  • Rewrite UI content in our voice and tone.
  • Apply component-specific guidance.
  • Respect character limits across dozens of UI patterns.
  • Recommend reusable content instead of creating new variations.
  • Explain why it suggested changes.
  • Coach designers toward stronger communication strategy.

It didn't just edit. It taught. If someone led with a feature instead of customer value, it explained why. If they repeated information in a title and description, it pointed it out. If they emphasized how instead of why, it redirected them.

The goal wasn't better prompts. It was better content design decisions.

Governance still mattered

Not every standard should be easy to bypass. Some patterns deliberately required content design judgment. Exclamation points were discouraged because they quickly made the product sound forced or juvenile. "We" shifted attention from users to the company. The AI explained these principles, suggested alternatives, and reinforced when content design review was required. It automated consistency—not judgment.

Beyond the bot

Once content design knowledge became structured, it powered much more than AI. The same work enabled:

  • Reusable notification architecture across email, SMS, and in-app messaging
  • Shared change management patterns
  • Feature awareness frameworks
  • Reusable promotional messaging
  • Repeatable content schemas used across product experiences

Marketing, product ops, researchers, and product designers all worked from the same playbook instead of inventing new approaches for every project.

The impact

More than 100 people across UX and partner organizations adopted the AI coach. Product designers—including many working in English as a second language—could confidently create production-ready UI content aligned with content design standards. Researchers and partners brought stronger work into review instead of blank pages.

Most importantly, the content design practice shifted its attention. Instead of spending hours applying standards manually, content designers spent more time in design reviews, influencing information architecture, supporting high-complexity work, and designing the next generation of systems for AI. The biggest change wasn't faster writing. It was changing where content design expertise created value.

More examples

This approach wasn't unique to content design. I used the same playbook with other disciplines.

Multimedia: Component illustration system. Built a reusable illustration library that let designers assemble graphics from standardized pieces instead of creating every illustration from scratch.

Multimedia: MVP-first production workflow. Every project started by defining the smallest valuable deliverable before investing in polished production. When priorities shifted and the team was pulled into executive work, stakeholders still received useful deliverables instead of waiting for unfinished work.

Principle: Don't build everything at once. Standards cut decisions; fewer decisions, faster output.

Step 4: Create the new normal

Persist and be patient. Then get out of the way.

The move

Step 3 is the most work. This one is the hardest. You need permission, credibility, and consistency. Every audience gets its own plan: designers, leaders, all of UX, partners.

How it played out

  • Content design went from ticket-taker to embedded partner on every product team, connected by a community of practice. There were tears, but it was the right move.
  • The people proof lives on My team: retention, promotions, and what the team says.

What the playbook delivered

Paychex UX content team, 2022–2024

  • Stopped work that cost more than it returned
  • Built leadership credibility through visible wins
  • Standards, templates, and tools that let partners help themselves
  • Left the organization permanently more capable

Lessons learned

I had conversations and gained verbal buy-in. But the culture change only worked for my designers who were willing to push for it themselves: asking to be included earlier, raising issues, pressing for reusable components. That advocacy can make you feel pesky, and it never stops. The more a designer was willing to push, the faster and further the culture shifted.
