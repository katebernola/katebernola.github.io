Step 1: Identify what doesn't add value

Do the math. Stop the work.

Case study

Stopping creation of Help Center videos

When I inherited the multimedia team, it was "the video team." The designers spent more than half their time making videos that mirrored Help Center articles. No one had ever done the math to analyze the actual value.

The math

Ready for a trip back to your 5th grade math class?

16 hours to create

×$120 per resource-hour

$1920 per video

$12 savings per contact avoided

×10% chance one view prevents a contact

$1.20 max value per view

$1920 per video

÷$1.20 max value per view

1600 views to break even

Current state

Help Center videos 237
Videos with 1600+ annual views 12
Videos below ROI threshold 95%

Each video took many hours to create. About half of that time came from people outside my team.

OrganizationHoursDollars
Company3,600$432k
Team1,800$216k

What we changed

  • We stopped taking requests for these videos.
  • Once each quarter, we looked at the most-read articles without videos.
  • We only made a video if the numbers showed it might be worth it.

Results

Before, the team created 3 videos per sprint. After, they produced 4 videos over the next few years. One was a C-suite ask, but the other three had data to support them.

Other ways it played out

  • Product design: We stopped designing for rare error cases. The PO shipped a generic message first and only invested more if the data showed it was needed.
  • Content design: Help Center articles made up the bulk of the Content Design team's work. I applied the same break-even math we used for Help Center videos. We cut production and upkeep by 90%.
  • Multimedia design: One 30-minute conversation paved the way to return demo videos to the original producers. All we had to do was create a reusable template.

Key insight

If a conversation doesn't solve it, show the math.

2

Step 2: Find surefire wins

Let your people shine. Fuel demand.

What I do

Seek out projects where the need is so obvious, sending in an expert feels like cheating.

Ways it played out

  • Multimedia design: When we completely overhauled our accountant portal, the content designer requested a few images. I embedded a multimedia designer on the project, enabling him to define visual hierarchy for the new portal, and suggest improvements that other designers didn't know were possible.
  • Content design: A 401(k) rebalancing flow had failed three rounds of usability testing. My designer reworked it, then rebuilt the retirement income estimator around how users think. Retirement made him their full-time content designer.
  • Content design: A payroll team asked for "content support" on a workflow nobody could explain. I proved the real issue was system design, and the team reprioritized toward the project that needed UX early.
  • Demand for content design grew across product teams.

Key insight

Credibility comes from solving important problems, not from taking every request.

Step 3: Make great work repeatable

Build tools and frameworks. Multiply impact.

Case study

Scaling expertise with AI

Our company acquired a new platform that nearly doubled our product footprint. At about the same time, content designers began moving into new UX leadership roles, so the team was shrinking.

We couldn't hire more designers.

Instead, we decided to make their expertise available to everyone.

The first agent: UI Writing Assistant

Like everyone else, our first instinct was to try AI. We loaded our standards into an AI writing agent and hoped it would coach people through creating UI content.

The results were disappointing.

The AI wrote closer to our voice than other people did, but it still made poor decisions. It missed character limits. It didn't understand when someone was using the wrong component. It couldn't explain why one approach was better than another. And it missed a lot.

Building the system

Years of guidance existed across wiki pages, design system documentation, and tribal knowledge. Content standards weren't integrated with the design system. And the design system documentation explained what to do, but not why.

So we stopped improving the AI, and started improving the knowledge architecture.

The content designers no longer reported to me. Each was embedded with a different product team, and everyone already had a full-time job. Even so, the practice still operated as one team.

I organized the work, divided ownership, and established a shared structure. The content designers drove the work themselves, reviewing each other's standards and holding one another accountable until every page followed the same framework.

Over three months, we transformed hundreds of scattered guidelines into one coherent knowledge system.

Growing the system

Once the structure existed, adding knowledge became straightforward.

We expanded the system beyond writing guidance to include communication strategy, reusable notification templates, content schemas, temporary messaging patterns, and component-level recommendations.

Instead of documenting rules, we documented decisions.

The second agent: content design coach

By the time Microsoft Copilot Enterprise became available, both the technology and our documentation had matured. Instead of acting like a copy generator, the AI behaved more like a content designer. It could:

  • Explain communication strategy
  • Recognize reusable patterns
  • Recommend better components
  • Tell people when they needed to consult a content designer

This wasn't a writing agent; it was a design coach. And it worked. Product designers produced stronger copy. Change management messaging prioritized risk mitigation. Marketing messaging led with value.

Results

More than 100 people across UX and partner organizations adopted the AI coach.

Content designers could invest expertise where it mattered. They developed strategy, improved information architecture, and shaped the integration of LLMs into our products.

Other ways it played out

  • Product design: Before creating a new solution, we began with a simple plan with elements that already existed. As we upgraded pieces, we always had something ready-to-ship. This enabled us to pivot on a dime, while also leaving dev with something to code.
  • Multimedia design: A reusable illustration library let designers assemble graphics from standardized pieces instead of creating every illustration from scratch.

Key insight

Don't scale people; scale expertise.

Step 4: Create the new normal

Persist and be patient. Then get out of the way.

Making it stick

Culture change doesn't happen because a leader says so. It takes permission, credibility, and consistency. Every audience needs a different plan: the team, peers, leaders, and partners.

Prepare your team to advocate for themselves. Looking across the organization, one pattern stood out: the more a designer pushed for a seat at the table, the further the culture shifted.

Ways it played out

  • Content designers went from ticket-takers to embedded partners on every product team, connected by a community of practice.
  • Product designers shifted from spending weeks in execution to providing data-based arguments that influenced the roadmap.
  • Multimedia design shifted from asset-creation to collaborative partnership, enabling the team to shape when and how multimedia was used.

Key insight

Leaders can create the conditions for change, but the team has to own it.
