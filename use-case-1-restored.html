Building and scaling a content design practice

Paychex UX content team, 2022–2024

Overview

A repeatable four-step framework for building design disciplines from next-to-nothing.

Step 1: Eliminate low-value work

Every team has some grunt work. This team had all grunt work. A little middle-school math proved my point: it didn't make cents for skilled designers to write Help content.

(See what I did there with cents?)

R1

I showed leaders the math, and they agreed to stop. Then I found other teams happy to take on the work that was still useful, just repetitive.

Help Center articles

8 hours to create and review × $120 per resource-hour $960 per article
$24 per contact avoided × 5% chance a view prevents one $1.20 max value per view
$960 ÷ $1.20 800 views to break even

Looking at actual usage, two-thirds of our articles never came close. We shifted intake from request-based to demand-based and redirected the team.

Help Center videos

At least 24 hours to make, review, and publish × $120 per resource-hour $2,880+ per video
Max value per view $1.20
$2,880 ÷ $1.20 2,400 views to break even

By that bar, we dropped support for more than 200 videos. Only 15 earned their place.

Demo videos

UX multimedia designer $100 per hour
Marketing multimedia designer $80 per hour

Sometimes you just need a cost comparison. This work started with marketing and moved to UX during a capacity crunch. We improved the overall design first (bumpers, a template, guidelines), then handed production back. The template made the work easy to do well at the lower rate.

Principle: Show the math. When leaders see that two-thirds of your output costs more than it returns, stopping becomes the obvious call.

Step 2: Show value

Freeing people up doesn't require new headcount. Point them at work where you know they'll make a difference.

R2

I picked a few big, visible projects where I knew we'd make a real difference. It wasn't long before people noticed, and demand started to grow.

Retirement: a pair of projects

Investment rebalancing had failed three rounds of usability testing. The design itself wasn't the problem: nobody understood what was happening. Rebalancing is complicated for an everyday 401(k) user with no retirement background. That made it the perfect testing ground:

  • Research showed content actually was the problem
  • Without the right content the project would fail; with it, success was clear
  • Product agreed the problem was big, and committed to structural changes if the content designer found the need

I assigned one of my content designers, with the next usability test a week away. He dove in, did the research to understand the content deeply, and reworked the design with the product designer. It worked, and we had measured qualitative data showing the impact.

But this looked a little too much like "bring us in at the last minute, and we'll fix everything for you." So I made a bargain: if he took this one, he also got a project that was early in development.

He picked up the retirement income estimator. He united two text-heavy screens into one. He replaced a wall of words with a simple chart that made the point better. He wrote a response for each scenario, from way too little saved to way too much. He pushed for a way to include funds from other accounts or from a partner, and made the case for inputs you could edit right on the results screen, no edit button needed.

R3

Then he pushed the product designer to iterate until those inputs clearly looked editable.

What he really did was reorder the workflow to match how users think. The value was obvious, and the retirement space was hooked. He became their full-time content designer.

R4

Mid-period rate change: diagnosing a systemic UX problem

A payroll feature called mid-period rate change was about to launch, and the team asked for "content support." The use case was legitimate. Some unions require raises on an employee's anniversary date. But the workflow was fragmented, inconsistent, and nearly impossible for users to understand.

Two different workflows existed for changing pay rates.

Standard raise: straightforward. Four steps, one straight line.

People → Compensation → Edit salary → Choose effective date (limited to pay-period start dates)

Mid-period: buried. Eight winding steps, a hidden entry point, and a final question nobody could explain.

Payroll → Worker → Salary (an undiscoverable click target) → 3-dot menu → Mid-period rate change → Enter amount → Select date → "How do you want this applied?"

Even the PO, PM, and product designer struggled to explain what that question meant. Usability tests weren't reliable because admins were testing with fake data. No real stakes, no real money. In real payroll, admins manually validate every number, often with calculators. Ambiguity isn't acceptable.

When asked to "just explain it," I mapped the IA required to make the interaction understandable. That's when the team revealed they couldn't modify the People screens. The logic had to stay in Payroll. The core issue wasn't content. It was the underlying system design. The complexity wasn't inherent to payroll; it was a byproduct of the technical implementation.

By reframing the conversation around frequency, impact, and IA timing, I redirected the team toward prioritizing the 100%-impact project that actually needed UX support early, rather than trying to patch an incomprehensible workflow with words.

R5

Why not just ask for new resources?

It's much harder to prove ROI for a resource you don't already have. If you don't have a team to redirect, other options:

  • Find someone who's interested, and make room on their plate
  • Find the least valuable work already being done, and test with that time instead
  • Do it yourself
  • Secure new headcount, full-time or contract

Principle: Find sure-fire wins. Avoid last-minute patches. Aim for "essential to success," not "superhero work."

Step 3: Scale value

Once you've proven impact, invest in systems. Standards, templates, and tools let one person's expertise multiply across the organization.

Standards

Content standards written for product designers (who don't have a writer on every project). Component standards clarifying when to use each element. 9 of 10 times a content designer got stuck, it was because the product designer picked the wrong component.

R6

A clear, shared source of truth means designers don't have to keep defending basic word choices. Content standards need to be thorough enough for content designers, but written for product designers. Most projects don't have a writer on hand. Publicly available standards from other companies are a good jump-start.

Strategies

One person leads each company-wide need (product-led growth, change management, communications). Light consulting, not a time sink.

Reusable content

Standard error messages, date/time formats, address formats, system labels. If you want to deviate, make your case to the team. If you want to change the standard, do the research and get buy-in.

Templates and tools

UMUX lites for measuring ease and usefulness. Notification templates. An AI writing assistant built on our content standards.

R7

A UMUX lite is a short, two-question survey that measures how easy and useful something feels to use.

R8

The AI writing assistant was built on our own content standards. Type in rough text, and it returns a UI-ready version. Future enhancements I was exploring:

  • Asking a few questions before responding (What stage is this design at? Will people reading this know what the term means? What's the one key thing to say?)
  • Naming the different pieces of knowledge packed into a draft
  • Reinforcing the single key message
  • Factoring in the user persona
  • Sending researchers a daily digest of unconfirmed assumptions to help build out the knowledge base
R9

Decision maps

Decision maps, or AI agents trained on them, help partners find answers fast, without waiting on a specialist.

Processes

Clear intake, definition of ready, and turnaround expectations.

Principle: Make work efficient, don't build everything at once. Standards and templates cut decisions, which speeds output.

Step 4: Change the culture

Assign consultants to each product team. Show up in their design reviews. Reinforce wins and progress continuously. Meet 1:1 with every designer. Communicate pre-launch and post-launch with every stakeholder group differently.

R10

Here's what I did at Paychex, which had a strong legacy culture. Tactics will vary a lot by org. Throughout, for every audience: I kept reinforcing concrete next steps and progress toward the big goal. I showed progress within each product space. Surveys that anyone could fill out measured how it was going. I watched the results and adjusted.

Content designers

Before launch, I worked with them to define the overall goal, then guided them in building a timeline and concrete next steps. After launch, I met weekly 1:1 with each designer to see their work, hear the barriers, and provide direction. We also met weekly as a team, for camaraderie and alignment.

Leadership, UX management, and leads

Before launch, I met one-off with each lead and manager. I shared ideas and listened to concerns. Then I refined the plan to address the key ones, worked the plan into departmental goals, and got buy-in to include it in personal goals at the manager and IC level. At launch, I rounded back with the group: the plan, the timeline, and what they needed to do.

All UX ICs

Before launch, I shared the vision of where things were headed. At launch: a department meeting, an email, a document on the team wiki, and a visit to each product group during a team meeting to discuss and answer questions. In each, I acknowledged how things worked today, shared what was changing, shared the wins from step 2 and what made them different, and defined the future state and the path to get there.

Partners

Before launch, I met 1:1 with peers to build relationships, learn pain points, and plant the seeds of what was coming. At launch: an email announcement, plus a follow-up where I walked through the high-level plan and answered questions. Any group that was directly impacted, or whose behavior needed to change, got its own message or meeting.

Longer term

To truly be part of the flow of work, content designers eventually needed to be embedded in each team, staying connected through a community of practice. There were tears, but it was the right move.

Principle: Step 3 is the most work. This is the hardest. You're asking people to think and work differently. You need permission, credibility, and consistency.

Outcomes

  • Stopped work that cost more than it returned, and redirected the team to high-value projects
  • Built leadership credibility through visible wins; demand for content design grew across product teams
  • Established standards, templates, and tools that let partners get what they need without waiting on a specialist
  • Left the organization permanently more capable

Lessons learned

The culture change only worked where my designers pushed for it themselves. They had to tell product designers to include them earlier. They had to raise issues constantly. They had to press for changes like reusable components, so text didn't need updating in a million places. That kind of advocacy can feel like ratting on the same people you're trying to collaborate with, and it never stops. Where a designer was willing to do it, the culture shifted. Where one wasn't, everything slid backward.

One content designer kept waiting for someone else to tell his product team to work differently, instead of asking, reminding, and pushing his way in. Nothing shifted in his space. He ended up working like a UI writer, got handed more Help Center articles than anyone, and without the strategy half of content design, he couldn't show his work was worth more than the articles. The lesson: a leader can clear the path and back the team up, but each designer has to do their own pushing.
